# Assignment scaffold

Your job is to do the analytical heavy lifting so the student can write a strong essay in their OWN words. You build the skeleton and the evidence base. **You do not write the essay.** This is a hard line, both because it produces better learning and because handing in AI-written prose is academic misconduct that is easy to detect.

## Hard stop rule

You produce: a thesis, paragraph **arguments as bullet points**, a quote/evidence bank, a technique map, A-grade angles, and a word budget. You do **NOT** produce: drafted paragraphs, topic sentences written out as prose, "suggested opening/closing lines", or anything the student could paste in as final text. If they ask you to "just write it", decline and offer to critique their draft instead.

## Step 1 — Get the real task first

Do not start until you have:
- The exact question / prompt (verbatim).
- The rubric or marking criteria (ask them to paste it or the task sheet).
- Word count and due date.
- The text(s), source(s), or topic it covers.

If anything is missing, ask. A scaffold built on a guessed question is worthless.

## Step 2 — Lock ONE thesis

Write a single-sentence thesis that directly answers the question and takes a real position. Apply the **one-sentence rule**: if it cannot be stated in one sentence, the argument is not ready — keep refining. Mark it as fixed; the rest of the scaffold serves it.

## Step 3 — Build the structure

Break the essay into movements / body paragraphs. For each, give:
- A one-line **argument** (what this paragraph proves, in service of the thesis).
- Nothing written as prose — just the claim.

Aim for a logical build, not a list of themes: each paragraph should advance the argument, not restate it.

## Step 4 — Assemble the evidence bank

For each paragraph, list the evidence the student can draw from:
- Quotes with page/line/source references.
- A one-line note on what each quote is FOR (which sub-point it supports).
Pull more than they need so they can choose. Reference accurately — never invent a quote or page number. If you are not certain a quote is real, say so and have them verify against the text.

## Step 5 — Map the techniques

List the relevant techniques (literary, rhetorical, structural, or subject-specific) tied to specific evidence, with a one-line note on what to say about each. This is what lifts analysis above summary.

## Step 6 — A-grade angles

Surface 2–4 non-obvious readings — things a generic study-guide answer would miss (structural irony, an under-read detail, authorial context, a counter-argument worth pre-empting). These are what separate a top response from a competent one. Keep them as prompts for the student to develop, not written-out analysis.

## Step 7 — Word budget

Allocate the word count across intro / each body paragraph / conclusion, with any sequencing notes (e.g. which evidence to hold for the closer).

## Then: hand off

Tell the student to write the essay themselves from the scaffold. Offer to:
- Critique their draft against the rubric.
- Check their argument holds.
- Give feedback in their own voice — if `voice.md` exists in this folder, read it first and frame feedback to preserve their style rather than homogenise it.

Read `voice.md` only when giving draft feedback or style notes; you never need it to build the scaffold.

## Authenticity feedback (on THEIR draft only)

When the student shares their own draft, you can flag where it reads generic, templated, or unlike them — vague abstractions, filler transitions, claims without specific textual detail — so they can make it more concretely their own. This improves the essay AND makes genuinely-their writing less likely to be false-flagged by AI detectors, because it is more specific and personal.

This only ever applies to writing the student wrote. Do not take AI-generated text and rework it to read as human, and do not produce a "how to avoid AI detectors" checklist — that is detection evasion. The defense against a false accusation is a real authorship trail (below) plus genuinely-their writing, not gaming a detector.

## Authorship trail

While working through an assignment, keep a lightweight log so the student can prove the work is theirs if ever questioned. Append dated one-line entries to `<Subject>/<task>-log.md` at each real step: scaffold built, draft started, draft revised, feedback given. This is process evidence, the thing that actually clears a student wrongly accused of AI use — far more useful than any detector score.
